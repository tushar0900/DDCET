Physical Quantities and Units

🗣
7 Fundamental quantities: Length (m), Mass (kg), Time (s), Temperature (K), Current (A), Luminous intensity (cd), Amount of substance (mol). Aa 7 yaad karva nu — baki sab inathi derive thaay chhe!
Physical Quantity: Any quantity that can be measured and used to describe or explain natural phenomena through mathematical laws. It is completely expressed by its magnitude and unit.

Examples: length, mass, time, force, temperature, electric current, pressure.
Non-examples (cannot be measured): happiness, love, pride, loyalty.

A physical quantity = numerical value × unit. Example: "150 metres" means the length is 150 times the unit 'metre'.

Interconversion of Units: MKS ↔ CGS

The key relationship: 1 m = 100 cm and 1 kg = 1000 g

Step-by-Step Conversions

1. Area: MKS → CGS
Area = L × L = L²
1 m = 100 cm = 10² cm
(1 m)² = (100 cm)² = (10²)² cm² = 10⁴ cm²
∴ 1 m² = 10⁴ cm²
2. Volume: MKS → CGS
Volume = L³
(1 m)³ = (100 cm)³ = 10⁶ cm³
∴ 1 m³ = 10⁶ cm³
3. Density: MKS → CGS
Density = mass/volume. Using 1 kg = 10³ g and 1 m³ = 10⁶ cm³:
1 kg/m³ = 10³ g / 10⁶ cm³ = 10⁻³ g/cm³
∴ 1 kg/m³ = 10⁻³ g/cm³
4. Acceleration (gravity): m/s² → cm/s²
g = 9.8 m/s²
9.8 m/s² = 9.8 × 100 cm/s² = 980 cm/s²
5. Force: Newton → Dyne
N = kg·m/s² and dyne = g·cm/s²
1 N = 1 kg × 1 m/s²
1 N = 10³ g × 10² cm/s² = 10⁵ g·cm/s²
∴ 1 N = 10⁵ dyne
6. Speed conversion: km/h → m/s
36 km/h = (36 × 1000 m) / (60 × 60 s) = 36000/3600 = 10 m/s

Vernier Caliper

Invented by: Pierre Vernier (French mathematician) in 1631. It allows measurement of 1/10th or 1/20th of 1 mm accurately.

Vernier Caliper — Instrument Parts & Scales

Least Count of Vernier Caliper

Least Count (LC): The smallest measurement that can be accurately measured by an instrument.

Principle: N divisions of Vernier Scale = (N−1) divisions of Main Scale

LC = 1 MSD − 1 VSD
LC = 1 MSD − [(N−1)/N] MSD = (1/N) MSD
LC = Value of 1 main scale division / Total vernier divisions
For standard vernier: 1 MSD = 1 mm, N = 10 → LC = 1/10 mm = 0.1 mm = 0.01 cm
Reading = Main Scale Reading + (Vernier coincidence × LC)
Where vernier coincidence = the Vernier division that exactly aligns with a main scale division
Normal Vernier calipers: LC = 0.1 mm or 0.05 mm or 0.02 mm. Smaller LC → Higher precision.
1.4

Micrometer Screw Gauge

Used to measure thickness of thin wire, paper, sheet or blade. Measures up to 0.001 mm (more precise than Vernier caliper which measures up to 0.01 cm).

Principle

Based on the screw gauge principle: when a screw completes one full rotation, its flat end (spindle) moves forward or backward by one pitch distance.

The linear displacement is proportional to the angular displacement.

PartFunction
U-Frame (Body)Structural frame holding all parts
AnvilFixed face — one end of the measurement gap
SpindleMoving face — moves towards/away from anvil
Main Scale (Sleeve)Linear scale on the hollow tube, reads mm
Thimble (Circular Scale)Rotating drum with 50 or 100 equal divisions
RatchetPrevents over-tightening by slipping when proper pressure is reached
Lock NutLocks reading in place

Pitch Distance

Pitch: The distance between two consecutive threads on the screw = the distance the spindle moves in one complete rotation.

Typical pitch = 0.5 mm or 1 mm.

How to Take a Reading — Step by Step

VERNIER CALIPER — Worked Reading Example
Suppose: Main scale reads 2.3 cm (2.3 is the last full cm/mm mark before the vernier 0). The 7th vernier division coincides with a main scale division. LC = 0.01 cm.
Step 1: Note Main Scale Reading (MSR) = 2.3 cm
Step 2: Note Vernier Coincidence (VC) = 7th division
Step 3: Reading = MSR + (VC × LC) = 2.3 + (7 × 0.01) = 2.3 + 0.07 = 2.37 cm
MICROMETER SCREW GAUGE — Worked Reading Example
Suppose: Barrel (sleeve) main scale reads 7.5 mm (7 full mm marks + 0.5 mm visible below datum). Thimble circular scale reads 24 (24th division aligned with datum line). Pitch = 0.5 mm, 50 divisions → LC = 0.01 mm.
Step 1: Main Scale Reading (MSR) = 7.5 mm (7 mm + visible 0.5 mm below line)
Step 2: Thimble reading = 24 divisions
Step 3: Reading = MSR + (thimble × LC) = 7.5 + (24 × 0.01) = 7.5 + 0.24 = 7.74 mm

Zero Error

Zero Error: When the jaws of Vernier caliper (or faces of micrometer) are fully closed and the zero of the vernier scale does NOT coincide with the zero of the main scale, there is a zero error.
TypeConditionCorrection
No zero errorVernier 0 coincides exactly with main scale 0No correction needed
Positive zero errorVernier 0 is to the RIGHT of main scale 0Subtract zero error from reading
Negative zero errorVernier 0 is to the LEFT of main scale 0Add the magnitude of zero error to reading
Correct Reading = Observed Reading − Zero Error
Zero error is positive (+) if the reading is MORE than true value, negative (−) if LESS. Always subtract zero error (with sign) from observed reading.
1.5

Errors in Measurement

Error of Measurement: The difference between the true value and the measured value of a quantity. No measurement is ever perfect.

Types of Errors

Systematic Errors

  • Always in one direction (always + or always −)
  • Causes are known → can be minimized
  • Sources: instrument defects, wrong technique, personal habits, external conditions
  • Example: worn metre scale, thermometer under armpit

Random Errors

  • Occur irregularly — sometimes +, sometimes −
  • Causes: unpredictable variations (temperature, pressure, voltage)
  • Minimized by: taking many readings and finding arithmetic mean

Sources of Systematic Errors

  1. Instrumental errors — imperfect design/calibration (e.g., metre scale worn at one end)
  2. Technique errors — wrong experimental procedure (e.g., thermometer in armpit gives lower reading than actual body temperature)
  3. Personal errors — observer's inexperience, parallax, misreading
  4. External causes — changes in temperature, pressure, humidity, wind

Estimation of Errors — Step by Step

Let a quantity be measured n times, giving values: a₁, a₂, a₃, … aₙ

Mean Value: ā = (a₁ + a₂ + … + aₙ) / n
The arithmetic mean is taken as the best (true) value of the quantity.
Absolute Error in each reading: Δaᵢ = ā − aᵢ
Can be positive or negative. Some readings overestimate, some underestimate.
Mean Absolute Error: Δā = (|Δa₁| + |Δa₂| + … + |Δaₙ|) / n
Always positive — we take magnitudes
Final Result: a = ā ± Δā
The true value lies between (ā − Δā) and (ā + Δā)
Relative Error = Δā / ā
Percentage Error (δa) = (Δā / ā) × 100 %
Worked Example — Refractive Index
Values: 1.29, 1.33, 1.34, 1.35, 1.32, 1.36, 1.30, 1.33
Mean: ā = (1.29+1.33+1.34+1.35+1.32+1.36+1.30+1.33)/8 = 10.62/8 = 1.3275 ≈ 1.33

Absolute errors: |0.04|, |0.00|, |0.01|, |0.02|, |0.01|, |0.03|, |0.03|, |0.00|
Sum = 0.14

Mean Absolute Error: Δā = 0.14/8 = 0.0175 ≈ 0.02

Relative Error = 0.02/1.33 = ±0.015
Percentage Error = 0.015 × 100 = ±1.5%

Propagation of Errors

When a derived quantity depends on measured quantities, how do errors combine?

If Z = A + B or Z = A − B: ΔZ = ±(ΔA + ΔB)
For addition AND subtraction — absolute errors ADD
If Z = A × B or Z = A/B: ΔZ/Z = ±(ΔA/A + ΔB/B)
For multiplication AND division — relative errors ADD
If Z = Aⁿ: ΔZ/Z = n × (ΔA/A)
For powers — relative error multiplied by the power
Example: Two capacitors in parallel
C₁ = (1.3 ± 0.1) μF, C₂ = (2.4 ± 0.2) μF
Cp = C₁ + C₂ = 1.3 + 2.4 = 3.7 μF
ΔCp = ±(ΔC₁ + ΔC₂) = ±(0.1 + 0.2) = ±0.3 μF
% error = (0.3/3.7) × 100 = ±8.1%
Result: Cp = (3.7 ± 0.3) μF ≡ 3.7 μF ± 8.1%
Example: Difference of lengths
l₁ = (5.62 ± 0.01) cm, l₂ = (4.34 ± 0.02) cm
l = l₁ − l₂ = 5.62 − 4.34 = 1.28 cm
Δl = ±(0.01 + 0.02) = ±0.03 cm
% error = (0.03/1.28) × 100 = ±2.34%
Example: g = 4π²L/T² — percentage error in g
Error in T = 2%, error in L = 1%
Δg/g = ΔL/L + 2(ΔT/T) = 1% + 2×2% = 5% (L appears to power 1, T appears to power 2 — each contributes)
For surface area of sphere (S = 4πr²): if error in volume is 9%, then error in surface area = (2/3) × 9% = 6%. Because V ∝ r³ and S ∝ r², so ΔS/S = (2/3) × ΔV/V.

Significant Figures

Significant figures indicate precision of a measurement.

Rules: All non-zero digits are significant. Zeros between non-zero digits are significant. Leading zeros are NOT significant. Trailing zeros after decimal are significant.
Example: Significant digits in 0.005308
0.005308 → digits 5, 3, 0, 8 are significant = 4 significant digits
Leading zeros (0.00) are not significant.
Example: Round 18.854 to 3 significant digits
18.854 → 3 sig figs → 18.9 (the 4th digit is 5, so round up)

Linear Motion — Velocity, Acceleration and Equations of Motion

🗣
3 equations of motion: (1) v = u + at, (2) s = ut + ½at², (3) v² = u² + 2as. Free fall ma a = g = 9.8 m/s². Aa 3 equations thi koi pun kinematics numerical solve thashe!
Motion: Change in position of an object with time. Linear (rectilinear) motion means motion along a straight line.

Key Terms

If a body moves with uniform velocity → acceleration = 0. If velocity changes → acceleration ≠ 0.

Equations of Motion (Kinematics) — Uniform Acceleration

These 3 equations apply when acceleration a is constant and the body moves in a straight line.

1st Equation: v = u + at
v = final velocity, u = initial velocity, a = acceleration, t = time
2nd Equation: s = ut + ½at²
s = displacement covered in time t
3rd Equation: v² = u² + 2as
Relates velocity, acceleration and displacement — no time needed
4th (derived): s = (u + v)t / 2
Average velocity × time. Useful when both u and v are known.
These equations are valid ONLY for: (1) constant acceleration, (2) straight-line motion, (3) uniform initial/final conditions.Equations of motion maatra constant acceleration ane straight-line motion mate j valid chhe — exam ma condition check karo! For free-falling bodies, use a = g = 9.8 m/s² (downward).

Free Fall Under Gravity

When a body falls freely under gravity (no air resistance), it has uniform acceleration a = g = 9.8 m/s² downward.

v = u + gt  |  h = ut + ½gt²  |  v² = u² + 2gh
h = height, g = 9.8 m/s². For a body dropped from rest: u = 0

Solved Examples — Kinematics

Example 1: A car starts from rest and reaches 72 km/h in 10 s. Find acceleration and distance.
Given: u = 0 (starts from rest), v = 72 km/h = 72×(5/18) = 20 m/s, t = 10 s
a = (v − u)/t = (20 − 0)/10 = 2 m/s²
s = ut + ½at² = 0×10 + ½×2×100 = 100 m
Example 2: A ball is thrown upward with 20 m/s. Find: (a) time to reach top, (b) max height.
At highest point v = 0. u = 20 m/s, a = −g = −9.8 m/s²
(a) v = u + at → 0 = 20 − 9.8t → t = 20/9.8 ≈ 2.04 s
(b) v² = u² + 2as → 0 = 400 − 2×9.8×h → h = 400/19.6 ≈ 20.4 m
Example 3: Brakes applied to a car moving at 30 m/s; stops in 100 m. Find retardation.
v = 0, u = 30 m/s, s = 100 m
v² = u² + 2as → 0 = 900 + 2a×100 → a = −900/200 = −4.5 m/s²
(Retardation = 4.5 m/s² opposite to direction of motion)

Force: An external effort (push or pull) that (1) produces or tries to produce motion in a body at rest, (2) stops or tries to stop a body in motion, (3) changes or tries to change the direction of motion.

  • Spring force

Non-Contact (Field) Forces

  • Magnetic force

Newton's Three Laws of Motion

1st Law — Law of Inertia
A body continues in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless an external force acts on it.

Inertia = tendency of a body to resist change in its state of motion. Mass is a measure of inertia — greater mass = greater inertia.
2nd Law — Law of Force
The rate of change of linear momentum of a body is proportional to the applied force, and change occurs in the direction of force.

F = ma = dp/dt
F = force, m = mass, a = acceleration, p = momentum
This gives us: 1 N = 1 kg × 1 m/s²
3rd Law — Law of Action-Reaction
For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. Forces always occur in pairs — action and reaction act on different bodies.

Example: When you push a wall, the wall pushes you back with equal force. When a rocket ejects gas backward, the gas pushes the rocket forward.

Solved Examples

Example 1: Find applied force
Mass = 5 kg, initial velocity u = 2 ms⁻¹, final velocity v = 3.5 ms⁻¹, time t = 2 s
a = (v − u)/t = (3.5 − 2)/2 = 1.5/2 = 0.75 ms⁻²
F = ma = 5 × 0.75 = 3.75 N
Example 2: Find acceleration
Force F = 15 N, mass m = 3 kg
a = F/m = 15/3 = 5 ms⁻²
Example 3: Find mass
Force F = 20 N, acceleration a = 5 ms⁻²
m = F/a = 20/5 = 4 kg

Linear Momentum and Impulse

🗣
Momentum = mass × velocity (p = mv). Law of conservation: total momentum before collision = total momentum after. Impulse = F × t = change in momentum. Aa concept bahu MCQ ma aave chhe!
Linear Momentum (p): The product of mass and velocity. It is a vector quantity — direction same as velocity.
p = m × v
SI unit: kg ms⁻¹  |  CGS unit: g cm s⁻¹

Important Properties of Momentum

  • When mass is constant: p ∝ v (momentum ∝ velocity)
  • When velocity is constant: p ∝ m (momentum ∝ mass)
  • If two bodies have equal momentum: M₁v₁ = M₂v₂ → v₂/v₁ = M₁/M₂
  • Heavier body → smaller velocity, lighter body → higher velocity (for equal momentum)

Impulse of Force

When a large force acts for a very short time, the body experiences an impulse. Example: hammer striking a nail, bat hitting a ball.
Impulse = Force × time = F × t
Impulse = Change in momentum = m·v₂ − m·v₁ = Δp
SI unit: N·s (newton-second) or kg ms⁻¹  |  CGS unit: dyne-second
Impulse is a VECTOR quantity.
Impulse = Change in momentum. This is the impulse-momentum theorem derived from Newton's 2nd law: F = dp/dt → F·dt = dp

Applications of Impulse

SituationHow impulse helps
Cricket player lowers hands while catchingIncreases time of impact → reduces force → hands not hurt
Person falls on sand/cushion (not cement)Longer time of momentum change → smaller force → less injury
China/glasswares wrapped in paper for packingPaper increases time → reduces average force → less breakage
Shock absorbers in vehiclesIncreases time of impact → reduces jerk force on rider
Buffers in train bogiesIncrease time of impact → reduce severe jerks during shunting

Circular Motion

Angular Velocity (ω)

Angular velocity: The angle (in radians) described by a rotating body per unit time. Symbol: ω (omega).
ω = θ/t = Angular displacement / time
Unit: radians per second (rad/s)

The angle θ (in radians) = arc AB / radius = S/r

Time Period (T) and Frequency (f)

T = Time for one complete revolution (seconds)
f = 1/T   (frequency in Hertz, Hz)
ω = 2π/T = 2πf
One Hz = one revolution per second

Angular Acceleration (α)

Rate of change of angular velocity.
α = dω/dt = (ω₂ − ω₁) / t   [rad/s²]

Relation Between Angular and Linear Velocity

v = rω
v = linear velocity (m/s), r = radius (m), ω = angular velocity (rad/s)
a = rα
a = linear (tangential) acceleration, α = angular acceleration
All particles of a rotating body have the SAME angular velocity. But linear velocity differs — particles farther from axis move faster: v = rω.

Centripetal Force and Centrifugal Force

Centripetal Force: The inward force directed towards the centre of circular path that keeps the body moving in a circle. It is a REAL force.
Centrifugal Force: The outward (pseudo) force felt by the object due to inertia. It is NOT a real force — it is a reaction to centripetal force by Newton's 3rd law. Equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.
Centripetal Force F = mv²/r = mω²r
m = mass, v = velocity, r = radius of circular path
Centripetal Acceleration a = v²/r = ω²r
Circular Motion — Forces on Object

Vertical Circular Motion — Tension in String

At LOWEST point: T = mg + mv²/r   (maximum)
At HIGHEST point: T = mv²/r − mg   (minimum)
Example: Mass 2 kg, radius 1 m, v = 2 m/s, g = 10 m/s²
At lowest point: T = mg + mv²/r = 2×10 + 2×4/1 = 20 + 8 = 28 N

2.4

Work, Energy and Power

Work

Work is done when a force causes displacement in the direction of the force.
W = F × d × cos θ
F = force (N), d = displacement (m), θ = angle between force and displacement
If force and displacement are in same direction: W = F × d
SI unit: Joule (J) = N·m  |  CGS: erg = dyne·cm
1 J = 10⁷ erg
Work is a SCALAR. Work is zero if: F=0, d=0, or θ=90° (force perpendicular to motion — e.g., a person carrying a bag while walking horizontally).

Energy

Energy is the capacity to do work. SI unit: Joule (J).

Kinetic Energy (KE)

  • Energy due to motion
KE = ½mv²
m = mass, v = velocity

Potential Energy (PE)

  • Energy stored due to position or configuration
PE = mgh
m = mass, g = 9.8 m/s², h = height
Work-Energy Theorem: W = ΔKE = ½mv² − ½mu²
Law of Conservation of Energy: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another. Total energy (KE + PE) remains constant in absence of non-conservative forces.
At any point: KE + PE = constant (for conservative systems)
½mv² + mgh = constant

Power

Power is the rate of doing work.
P = W/t = F × v
SI unit: Watt (W) = J/s  |  1 horsepower = 746 W
1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J (commercial unit of energy)

Electric Charge

Every atom contains electrons (negative), protons (positive), and neutrons (neutral). The property of particles due to which electric force exists between them is called electric charge.
9.1 × 10⁻³¹ kg
Mass of electron
1.6 × 10⁻²⁷ kg
Mass of proton/neutron

Fundamental charge (electron/proton)

Electrons in 1 Coulomb

Only electrons are transferred during charging. The substance that receives electrons becomes negatively charged; the one that loses electrons becomes positively charged. Every object is neutral by default.

n = 1/e = 1/(1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹) = 6.25 × 10¹⁸ electrons per coulomb
SI unit of charge: Coulomb (C)
Like charges repel. Unlike charges attract. Electric force is 10⁴³ times greater than gravitational force between two electrons!
3.2

Coulomb's Law

Coulomb's Law: The electric force between two stationary point charges is directly proportional to the product of their charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
F = k · q₁q₂ / r²
k = 1/(4πε₀) = 8.9875 × 10⁹ ≈ 9 × 10⁹ N·m²·C⁻²
ε₀ = permittivity of free space = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² C²/Nm²
In CGS: k = 1 dyne·cm²/esu²
Force is along the line joining the two charges.
In a medium: Fₘ = (1/4πε) · q₁q₂/r²
F/Fₘ = ε/ε₀ = εᵣ = K (dielectric constant)
Relative permittivity εᵣ > 1 always, so Fₘ < F (force decreases in a medium)
When distance is doubled → force becomes 1/4th. When charges are doubled → force becomes 4 times.
Example: Total charge on 20 extra electrons
q = n × e = 20 × (−1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹) = −3.2 × 10⁻¹⁸ C
3.3

Electric Field, Potential and Flux

Electric Field (E)

The force per unit positive test charge placed at a point. It is the effect of a charge felt in the surrounding space.
E = F / q₀
E = kQ / r² = (1/4πε₀) · Q/r²
SI unit: N/C or V/m
Electric field is a VECTOR quantity.

Properties of Electric Field Lines

  1. Start from positive charges, end at negative charges
  2. Tangent at any point = direction of electric field at that point
  3. Two field lines NEVER intersect (would imply two directions at one point)
  4. Closer (denser) lines = stronger field; farther apart = weaker field
  5. Uniform field lines are parallel and equidistant
  6. Field lines are imaginary but electric field is real
  7. Field lines are always perpendicular to the conducting surface
  8. Field lines do NOT form closed paths

Electric Potential (V)

Work done against the electric field in bringing a unit positive charge from infinity to a point in the field.
V = W / q₀
VA = −kQ/r
Unit: Volt (V) = Joule/Coulomb
1 Volt = 1 J/C
Electric potential is a SCALAR quantity.
Potential Difference: VB − VA = −k·Q/rAB = −WAB/q₀

Electric Flux (φ)

The number of electric field lines passing perpendicularly through a given surface area.
φ = E · A · cos θ
E = electric field, A = area, θ = angle between E and normal to surface
SI unit: V·m or N·m²/C
Electric flux is a SCALAR.
If θ = 0°: φ = EA (maximum)  |  If θ = 90°: φ = 0 (parallel)

Capacitance and Capacitors

Heat Capacity and Specific Heat

Heat Capacity (Hc)

The ratio of heat given to a body to the corresponding change in its temperature.
Hc = Q / ΔT
SI unit: J/K  |  Practical: cal/°C or cal/K

Heat capacity depends on both the material AND mass of the body.

Specific Heat (c)

The quantity of heat required per unit mass to raise the temperature of a body by 1 unit. It depends only on the material, NOT the mass.
c = ΔQ / (m × ΔT)  →  ΔQ = m·c·ΔT
SI unit: J/(kg·K)  |  CGS: cal/(g·°C)
specific heat = Heat capacity / mass

4.5

Thermal Conductivity

Thermal Conductivity (K): The intrinsic ability of a solid to conduct heat through it. Different substances have different thermal conductivities.

Good conductors: copper, silver, metals. Bad conductors (insulators): glass, wood, rubber, air.

Formula for Heat Conduction (Steady State)

ΔQ/Δt = K · A · (T₁ − T₂) / d
ΔQ/Δt = rate of heat flow (W)
K = coefficient of thermal conductivity
A = cross-sectional area (m²)
T₁ − T₂ = temperature difference (°C or K)
d = thickness / distance between faces (m)
SI unit of K: W·m⁻¹·K⁻¹ (= J·s⁻¹·m⁻¹·K⁻¹)
CGS unit: cal·s⁻¹·cm⁻¹·°C⁻¹
Temperature Gradient: The rate of fall of temperature per unit distance in the direction of heat flow = ΔT/Δx.

Steady State: When temperature of every cross-section becomes constant and there is no more heat absorption. Only thermal conductivity matters (not specific heat) in steady state.
Example 1: Copper rod
Copper rod: length 19 cm = 0.19 m, area = 0.785 cm² = 785×10⁻⁷ m², T₁=100°C, T₂=30°C, t=10 min=600 s, K=380 W/mK
Q = KA(T₁−T₂)t/d = 380 × 785×10⁻⁷ × 70 × 600 / 0.19
Q = 380 × 785 × 70 × 600 × 10⁻⁷ / 0.19 = 6594 J ≈ 6.59 kJ
Example 2: Glass window
A = 0.5 m², d = 6 mm = 6×10⁻³ m, T inside = 23°C, T outside = 2°C, K = 1.0 W/mK, t = 1 hr = 3600 s
Q = 1.0 × 0.5 × (23−2) × 3600 / (6×10⁻³)
Q = 0.5 × 21 × 3600 / 0.006 = 37800/0.006 = 6,300,000 J = 63 × 10⁵ J

Linear Thermal Expansion

When a solid is heated, its length increases. The fractional change in length per degree Celsius rise in temperature is the coefficient of linear expansion (α).
L = L₀(1 + α·ΔT)
ΔL = L₀ · α · ΔT
L₀ = original length, L = new length, ΔT = temperature change
α = coefficient of linear expansion (per °C or per K)
Unit of α: °C⁻¹ or K⁻¹
Area Expansion: A = A₀(1 + β·ΔT) where β = 2α
Volume Expansion: V = V₀(1 + γ·ΔT) where γ = 3α
β = coefficient of superficial (area) expansion = 2α
γ = coefficient of cubical (volume) expansion = 3α
Relationship: α : β : γ = 1 : 2 : 3
Bimetallic strips bend when heated because two metals have different coefficients of linear expansion — one expands more, causing the strip to curve. Used in thermostats and bimetallic thermometers.

5.3

Refraction, Refractive Index and Snell's Law

Refraction: The bending of light when it passes from one medium to another. Occurs because the speed of light is different in different media.

Laws of Reflection (Important Prerequisite)

Reflection: Bouncing back of light (or any wave) from a surface.
  1. The angle of incidence (i) = angle of reflection (r). Both measured from the normal to the surface at the point of incidence.
  2. The incident ray, reflected ray, and normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.
Laws of reflection apply to ALL types of mirrors (plane, concave, convex) and to all waves (light, sound, water waves). Total Internal Reflection also obeys these same laws of reflection.

Optically rarer medium (like air): higher speed of light. Optically denser medium (like glass): lower speed of light.

Laws of Refraction

  1. The incident ray, refracted ray, and normal at the point of incidence — all lie in the same plane.
  2. Incident ray and refracted ray are on opposite sides of the normal.
  3. Going rarer→denser: bends TOWARDS normal (angle of refraction < angle of incidence: r < i)
  4. Going denser→rarer: bends AWAY from normal (r > i)
  5. A ray hitting normal (i=0°) passes straight through without bending.
  6. Snell's Law: sin i / sin r = constant = refractive index

Refractive Index and Snell's Law

μ = η = sin i / sin r = constant (Snell's Law)
Relative refractive index of medium 2 w.r.t. medium 1: μ₂₁ = sin i / sin r = v₁/v₂
v₁, v₂ = speeds of light in medium 1 and medium 2
Symbol: μ (mu) or η (eta) or n

Diamond has the highest refractive index among common substances. This is why it sparkles — total internal reflection occurs easily due to small critical angle (24°).

  • Objects at the bottom of water appear shallower than they are
  • Difficult to catch a fish — its position appears different from actual

5.4

Total Internal Reflection (TIR) and Critical Angle

Total Internal Reflection (TIR): When light travels from a denser medium to a rarer medium, and the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle, the light is completely reflected back into the denser medium — none passes through.

Critical Angle (θc)

The angle of incidence (in the denser medium) for which the angle of refraction becomes exactly 90° (refracted ray travels along the boundary).
sin θc = nᵣ / nᵢ = n₂/n₁
θc = sin⁻¹(nᵣ/nᵢ)
nᵢ = refractive index of denser (incident) medium
nᵣ = refractive index of rarer medium
For water-air: θc = sin⁻¹(1/1.33) ≈ 48.6°
For crown glass-air: θc = sin⁻¹(1/1.52) ≈ 41.14°
For diamond-air: θc = sin⁻¹(1/2.42) ≈ 24°
Derivation: nᵢ sin θc = nᵣ sin 90° = nᵣ (since sin 90° = 1)
∴ sin θc = nᵣ/nᵢ

Necessary Conditions for TIR

  1. Light must travel from denser medium → rarer medium
  2. Angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle (i > θc)
TIR does NOT occur when light goes from rarer to denser medium. Higher refractive index → smaller critical angle → easier TIR.

5.5

Optical Fiber

Optical Fiber: A thin, flexible strand of high-quality glass, quartz, or plastic that transmits light (and information) over long distances using Total Internal Reflection.

Construction

PartDescription
CoreCentral part — high refractive index (n₁). Light travels through here. Diameter: ~8-10 μm (single mode) or 50-200 μm (multimode)
CladdingLayer surrounding core — lower refractive index (n₂). Ensures TIR at core-cladding boundary. Condition: n₁ > n₂
Outer jacketProtective plastic coating
Condition for light propagation: n₁ > n₂ (core denser than cladding)
Numerical Aperture (NA) = √(n₁² − n₂²)
NA indicates the light-gathering capacity of the fiber. Higher NA = more light accepted.

How Light Travels in Optical Fiber

  1. Light enters one end of the fiber at the acceptance angle
  2. Hits core-cladding boundary at an angle greater than critical angle
  3. Undergoes TIR — bounces along the fiber
  4. Even if fiber is curved or twisted, TIR continues
  5. Light emerges from the other end
Even if angle of incidence exceeds the acceptance angle at entry, light will NOT propagate in the fiber.

Types of Optical Fibers

TypeDescription
Single-mode step-index fiberCore diameter ~8-10 μm. One path for light. Used in long-distance communication. Most widely used.
Multimode step-index fiberCore diameter ~50-200 μm. Multiple paths. More signal loss.
Multimode graded-index fiberRefractive index decreases gradually from center. Reduces dispersion.

Applications of Optical Fiber

  • Telecommunications: Internet cables, phone lines — carry signals as light pulses at high speed. Optical fibers carry far more information than copper wires (wider bandwidth).
  • Medical (Endoscopy): Flexible fiber bundles allow doctors to view inside the body without surgery. Used in colonoscopy, bronchoscopy, etc.
  • Military and sensing: Secure communication, fiber optic sensors
  • Broadcasting: Cable TV, data transmission

Estimated lifespan of optical fiber: 20–30 years. Phenomenon responsible for signal loss: scattering and absorption.

LASER

LASER = Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation

Characteristics of Laser Light

#PropertyExplanation
1MonochromaticSingle wavelength (single frequency/colour)
2CoherentAll photons are in the same phase (or constant phase difference)
3Directional (Unidirectional)Travels in one straight direction with very little divergence — beam cross-section barely changes over long distances
4High Intensity & BrightnessEnergy concentrated in a narrow beam — extremely bright
5Parallel raysRays in laser beam are parallel to each other
6Stimulated emissionProduced by stimulated (induced) emission — NOT spontaneous emission

Laser vs Ordinary Light

Ordinary Light (Bulb)

  • Spreads in ALL directions (divergent)
  • Intensity decreases rapidly with distance
  • Polychromatic (many wavelengths)
  • Incoherent (photons in different phases)
  • Low intensity
  • Cannot be focused to a very tiny spot

LASER

  • Travels in ONE direction (non-divergent)
  • Intensity remains almost constant
  • Monochromatic (single wavelength)
  • Coherent (all photons in phase)
  • Very high intensity
  • Can be focused to microscopic spot

Applications of LASER

  • Metal cutting and hole drilling (industrial)
  • Welding (precise, clean)
  • Eye surgery (LASIK), cancer treatment
  • Barcode scanners in shops
  • CD/DVD read/write laser
  • Optical mouse (computer)
  • Fiber optic communication (laser as light source)
  • Surveying, remote sensing, LIDAR
  • Testing physical and chemical properties of materials
5.7

Echo, Reverberation and Sabine's Formula

Echo

Echo: A repetition of sound heard after reflection from a distant hard surface. The original sound and its echo are heard as separate sounds.
Condition for echo: Minimum distance from reflecting surface = v×t/2
For distinct echo: time gap ≥ 1/10 s (0.1 s — minimum persistence of hearing)
At 343 m/s (speed of sound at room temperature):
Min. distance = 343 × 0.1/2 ≈ 17 m

Reverberation

Reverberation: The persistence of sound in an enclosed space after the source has stopped. It occurs due to multiple reflections of sound from walls, ceiling, floor, etc.

In echo — the reflected sound arrives AFTER the original has ended (>0.1 s gap).
In reverberation — reflected sounds overlap with the original, prolonging it.

Reverberation Time (RT or T): The time taken for sound intensity to fall to one millionth (10⁻⁶) of its original value after the source stops. Equivalently, time for sound level to fall by 60 dB.
Optimal reverberation time for speech: 0.5–1.0 s. For music: 1.5–2.5 s. Too much reverberation → sounds blend, unclear speech. Too little → sounds dead, flat.

Sabine's Formula

T = 0.161 × V / A
T = reverberation time (seconds)
V = volume of the room (m³)
A = total absorption in the room (sabins) = Σ(aᵢ × Sᵢ)
aᵢ = absorption coefficient of ith surface material
Sᵢ = area of ith surface (m²)
Absorption coefficient (a) = Sound energy absorbed / Sound energy incident
Ranges from 0 (perfect reflection) to 1 (complete absorption)
Unit of total absorption A: sabin (1 sabin = 1 m² of perfectly absorbing surface)
Understanding Sabine's Formula
A lecture hall has volume V = 3000 m³ and total absorption A = 200 sabins.
T = 0.161 × 3000 / 200 = 483/200 = 2.415 seconds
(This is too high for a lecture hall — would need more absorbing material to reduce it to ~1 s)

Acoustic Design of Auditoriums

  • Walls covered with sound-absorbing materials (felt, carpets, perforated boards)
  • Curved reflecting surfaces direct sound to all areas
  • No sharp echoes from back wall — use sound absorbers
  • Volume chosen carefully for required reverberation time
  • Audience itself contributes to absorption
