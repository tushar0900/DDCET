What is an Ecosystem?
🗣
"Eco" = Greek ma "oikos" = home. Ecosystem = ek jagah jya biotic (jivit) ane abiotic (nir-jiv) vastu interact kare chhe. Sun energy input aape chhe, decomposers energy/nutrients return kare chhe — circular process!

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms (plants, animals, microbes) interacting with each other and with their non-living (physical) environment as a system. Earth itself is the largest ecosystem.

The word "eco" comes from Greek oikos meaning "home." An ecosystem is essentially a home where living and non-living things interact constantly. Energy flows and matter cycles within it.

Eco = Greek ma "home" — ecosystem matlab saad bhashama: jivit ane nir-jiv vastu na gharna aapas ma interaction!

Components of Ecosystem

Every ecosystem has two types of components:

🏗️ (A) Structural Components

  • Biotic – living things (plants, animals, microbes)
  • Abiotic – non-living things (air, water, soil, sunlight, temperature)

⚙️ (B) Functional Components

  • Food chain
  • Food web
  • Bio-Geo Chemical Cycles (Carbon cycle, Nitrogen cycle, Water cycle)
Biotic Components (Living Things)

Biotic = all living organisms in an ecosystem. Classified into 3 groups based on their role:

1. Producers (Autotrophs)

Organisms that can make their own food. They are the building blocks of every ecosystem.

Producers

  • Green plants & Algae → use sunlight to convert CO₂ + water → glucose + O₂ (Photosynthesis)
  • Some bacteria → use energy from inorganic chemicals to make food (Chemosynthesis)
Photosynthesis: CO₂ + H₂O + Sunlight → Glucose + O₂

The most important element in the composition of biotic components is Carbon (C). Organisms contain about 49% carbon. In DNA and RNA, the important element is Phosphorous (P).

2. Consumers (Heterotrophs)

Organisms that cannot make their own food — they get energy by eating other organisms. Also called heterotrophs.

Type Also Called Feeds On Examples
Herbivores Primary / 1st order consumers Plants only Cow, deer, goat, grasshopper
Carnivores Secondary / 2nd order consumers Animals (herbivores) Fox, snake, birds eating insects, hawks
Tertiary consumers 3rd order consumers Secondary consumers Lion, tiger
Omnivores Opportunistic feeders / top-level carnivores Plants AND animals Humans, frog

In a food web, Humans are both primary and secondary consumers (omnivores). They eat plants (primary) and animals (secondary). Answer = (c).

3. Decomposers (Reducers / Saprotrophs)

Microorganisms that break down dead organic material of producers and consumers into simpler substances. They recycle nutrients back into the soil.

Decomposers

  • Bacteria – attack animal tissues
  • Fungi – attack plant tissues
  • Earthworms, Termites

Also called "reducers" because they reduce the volume of organic material. Also called saprotrophs.

4. Scavengers (Detritivores)

Animals that feed on dead bodies of other organisms. Example: Earthworms, termites. Also called saprotrophs.

In a food chain, energy flows from trophic level-1 (producers) to higher levels (1→4). Energy is LOST at each step (~90% lost, only 10% passed on). This is the 10% law.

Food Web

A food web is a complex network of multiple interconnected food chains. It gives a more realistic picture of feeding relationships in an ecosystem.

Pyramid of Numbers

A pyramid of numbers shows the number of organisms at each trophic level. At the base (producers), population is maximum. As you go up, numbers decrease.

In pyramid of numbers, Producers have maximum population. This is because energy decreases at every level, so fewer organisms can be supported at higher levels.

Pollution & Its Types

Air · Water · Noise · Solid Waste · Plastic · E-Waste
Key Topic
What is Environmental Pollution?

Environmental pollution = any undesirable change in physical, chemical, or biological characteristics of any component of environment (air, water, land) that causes harmful effects on various life forms or property.

Pollution dates back to when humans first discovered fire. Burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) releases poisonous gases.

🌋 Natural Causes

  • Volcanic eruptions
  • Earthquakes
  • Storms and dust
  • UV radiations
  • Decay of organic matter (releases H₂S)

🏭 Man-made Causes

  • Commercial & industrial activities
  • Over population, Urbanisation
  • Insecticides, pesticides
  • Use of vehicles, Mining
  • Nuclear explosions
  • Construction of buildings, roads, dams
Types of Environmental Pollution
💧 Water pollution
💨 Air pollution
🏜️ Land pollution
🔊 Noise pollution
☢️ Radioactive pollution
🌡️ Thermal pollution
Pollutant

A pollutant = any substance present in environment in concentration that alters quality of environment and affects living things adversely. NOT all pollutants are harmful if present in small amounts (e.g., nitrogen, phosphorus, sulphur in small amounts increase soil fertility).

Classification of Pollutants

♻️ Biodegradable Pollutants

Degraded naturally by bacteria. Examples:

  • Municipal waste water (sewage)
  • Wood, Paper, Garbage, Cardboard

⛔ Non-biodegradable Pollutants

Cannot be broken down naturally. Examples:

  • DDT, Plastics, Aluminium
  • Heavy metals like lead, mercury

MCQ shortcut: Paper is biodegradable. DDT, Plastic, Aluminium are NOT biodegradable.

Air Pollution
🗣
Air pollution na major sources: Vehicles (CO, NOx), Industries (SO₂), Burning (particulate matter). CO = colorless, odorless, deadly! CO₂ na vadhara thi greenhouse effect. CFC = ozone deplete kare chhe — refrigerators ma vaparay!

Definition (IS:4167-1966): "Air pollution is the presence in ambient atmosphere of substances, generally resulting from human activity, in sufficient concentration and for sufficient time that causes harmful effects on humans, plants and animals."

Scales of Air Pollution

Indoor (Micro scale)

Inside buildings, homes. E.g., cooking smoke.

Outdoor / Ambient (Meso scale)

Local outdoor area. E.g., city smog.

Global (Macro scale)

Global level. E.g., ozone depletion, greenhouse effect.

Sources of Air Pollution

🌿 Natural Sources

  • Atmospheric chemical reactions
  • Volcanic eruptions (gases + ash)
  • Forest fires (smoke + greenhouse gases)
  • Dust storms, electric storms
  • Marsh gases (decaying vegetation)
  • Pollen (from trees, grasses, weeds)
  • Salt spray from oceans
  • Microorganisms (algae, fungi, bacteria)
  • Radioactive substances (uranium, radium, thorium)

🏭 Man-made Sources

  • Combustion of fuel – coal, wood, oil, LPG
  • Automobile exhaust – CO (carbon monoxide)
  • Industries – iron/steel, refineries, chemical plants
  • Thermal power plants – SO₂
  • Nuclear power plants – radioactive fly ash
  • Agricultural activities – pesticides
  • Construction materials – bricks, cement dust
  • Sanitation system – garbage, foul gases
  • Mining, Nuclear explosions, Aircraft
  • Waste water treatment plants

CFC (Chlorofluorocarbon) – produced from refrigerators, air conditioners, and paints. Contains Carbon, Hydrogen, Chlorine and Fluorine. Major cause of ozone layer depletion.

Minamata disease is caused by Mercury poisoning. Bhopal Gas Tragedy (Dec 1984) was caused by Methyl Isocyanate (MIC) leaked from Union Carbide company.

Effects on Plants
  • Air pollutants enter through stomata (leaf pores), destroy chlorophyll, affect photosynthesis
  • Pollutants erode the waxy coating of leaves called cuticle → leaf drop
  • Particulates (dust, fog, soot) block stomata → reduce transpiration rate
  • SO₂ affects marble, limestone, roofing, paper, textiles, monuments
Effects on Climate

CO₂ and greenhouse gases → temperature rises → melting of ice caps and glaciers → rise in sea level. About 50% of greenhouse effect is attributed to CO₂.

Effects on Animals
  • Lead poisoning near smelters → paralysis, difficulty breathing, loss of appetite
  • Arsenic poisoning → salivation, vomiting, abnormal temperature, death
  • Fluorine toxicity in cattle and sheep → lack of appetite, diarrhoea, muscle weakness, death

Photochemical Smog

Formed when NOₓ + Hydrocarbons + Sunlight react in atmosphere → forms PAN (Peroxyacetyl Nitrate).

NOₓ + Hydrocarbons + Sunlight → PAN (Photochemical Smog)

Main product of photochemical smog = PAN. Gas harmful to Taj Mahal = SO₂ (sulphur dioxide). Air pollutant that reduces oxygen-carrying capacity of blood = CO (Carbon Monoxide).

Control of Air Pollution
  1. Use LPG/CNG instead of diesel/petrol in automobiles
  2. Grow more trees (trees absorb atmospheric CO₂)
  3. Do not burn plastic wastes
  4. Use bicycles; use public transport
  5. Process changes in industries (e.g., wash coal before pulverisation)
  6. Control dust in cement plants (rotary kiln)
  7. Fix catalytic converters in vehicles
  8. Reduce use of pesticides and chemical fertilisers
  9. Use electrostatic precipitators in thermal power plants
  10. Use controlling equipment: cyclone separator, fabric filter, wet scrubber
Air Pollution Control Equipment
1. Gravitational settling chamber
2. Cyclone separator
3. Electrostatic precipitators
4. Fabric filters
5. Wet scrubbers

In a cyclone separator, the force used to remove particulate matter from gas is centrifugal force.

Water Pollution

Any physical, biological, or chemical change in water quality that adversely affects living organisms or makes water unsuitable for use = water pollution.

Signs of Polluted Water
  • Bad taste or odour
  • Offensive odours from rivers, lakes, oceans
  • Reduction in number of fish / aquatic life
  • Oil or grease floating on surface
  • Unchecked growth of aquatic weeds
  • Presence of colour due to organic matter
Major Sources of Water Pollution
  • Sewage (discharge of sewers and drains)
  • Industrial effluents (chemical, dye, paper, tannery)
  • Intensive use of chemical fertilisers
  • Use of insecticides in fields
  • Synthetic detergents
  • Oil spillage during drilling and shipment
  • Discharge from nuclear power plants / research centres
  • Radioactive ash from nuclear explosions
  • Dead animals thrown in water bodies
  • Waste heat from industrial discharge and thermal plants

BOD = Biochemical Oxygen Demand – measures the amount of organic (carbonic) matter in domestic sewage. Higher BOD = more polluted water. pH of acidic water = less than 7.

Noise Pollution

🔊 Sound vs Noise

SoundNoise
What we hearUnpleasant, unwanted sound
Constant pitch (frequency)Variable pitch
Periodic motionNon-periodic motion
Meaningful communicationNo meaningful communication
Unit: Hertz (Hz)Unit: Decibel (dB)

📊 Measurement

Intensity of sound = sound energy received per second per unit area.

dB = 10 log₁₀ (I / I₀)

Where I₀ = 10⁻¹² W/m² (reference intensity)

Acceptable noise in urban residential area = 35–45 dBA

Repeated sound of 150 dB reduces hearing capacity.

Effects of Noise Pollution

Physical Effects

  • Loss of hearing (>80–90 dBA for 8+ hrs/day)
  • Total deafness (acoustic trauma)
  • Damage to ear drum from sudden loud sounds

Physiological Effects

  • Headache, nausea
  • Dizziness, gastric ulcers
  • High heart beat rate
  • Blood pressure and sugar fluctuations

Psychological Effects

  • Annoyance, insomnia (sleep interference)
  • Depression, fatigue
  • Mental stress, speech interference
  • Decrease in efficiency
Control of Noise Pollution
  1. Control at receiver's end – ear plugs, ear muffs, noise helmets, headphones
  2. Suppression at source – soundproof chambers, proper maintenance, sound-absorbing materials, silencers, quieter machines, vibration damping (rubber, cork, neoprene under machines)
  3. Acoustic zoning – silence zones near hospitals, schools, residential areas; keep industrial areas, bus terminals, railway stations, aerodromes away from residential areas
  4. Planting trees – green trees along roadsides reduce noise
  5. Sound insulation – acoustical tiles, perforated plywood in recording rooms
  6. Legislative measures – Noise Pollution Act, restrict loud speakers near silence zones
Solid Waste Management (SWM)

Solid waste = all solid and semi-solid materials discarded by a community. Arises from human and animal activities.

Types of Solid Waste

1. Refuse

General term for all rejected/worthless solid wastes. Includes garbage + rubbish + ashes + industrial wastes. Does NOT include body waste (excreta).

2. Garbage

Putrescible organic waste from kitchens, hotels, restaurants (vegetable/fruit peelings, waste food). Density: 450–900 kg/m³. Decomposes quickly.

3. Rubbish

Non-putrescible wastes excluding ashes. Paper, rags, glass/plastic bottles, broken crockery, furniture. Density: 50–400 kg/m³.

4. Ashes

Incombustible waste from houses, industries, furnaces, chulhas. Density: 700–850 kg/m³.

More Definitions

5. Putrefaction

Anaerobic decomposition of organic matter by anaerobic/facultative bacteria in absence of oxygen.

6. Leachate

Liquid that has travelled through solid waste and has dissolved materials. Non-biodegradable dumped waste + rain water → coloured liquid = leachate.

Municipal Solid Waste (MSW)

Waste from domestic and commercial activities. Also called "refuse." Includes garbage, rubbish, ashes, dust, construction wastes, dead animals.

India MSW: ~65 million tons/year
Projected by 2031: ~125 million tons
Land required: 1250 hectares/year
Objectives of SWM
  1. Remove discarded materials from inhabited places in timely manner
  2. Prevent spread of disease
  3. Minimise likelihood of fires
  4. Reduce aesthetic insults from putrifying organic matter
  5. Dispose refuse minimising hazards to environment
Three Basic Functional Elements of SWM
1. Collection
2. Disposal
3. Recycling/Reutilisation
Plastic Waste & Its Hazards

Plastics = synthetic organic polymers. Used in everything from water bottles to medical supplies. Despite initial assumptions of being harmless, plastic disposal has caused major environmental problems.

Global production 2018: 380 million tonnes
Since 1950: 6.3 billion tonnes produced
Only 9% recycled, 12% incinerated
Yearly in oceans: 13 million tonnes
Marine lives killed: ~100,000/year
Toxic Constituents in Plastics
Phthalates
Poly-fluorinated chemicals
Bisphenol A (BPA)
Brominated flame retardants
Antimony trioxide
Environmental Effects of Plastic Waste
  • Environmental natural beauty deterioration
  • Entanglement and death of aquatic organisms (260+ marine species)
  • Sewage system blockage → mosquito breeding
  • Reduction in water percolation and soil aeration → reduced agricultural productivity
  • Chlorinated plastics leach toxic chemicals into soil → contaminate groundwater
  • Methane (dangerous greenhouse gas) released during biodegradation of plastics
  • Plastics in oceans release polystyrene and BPA → water pollution
  • Open burning → dioxins, PCBs, furans → respiratory disorders
  • Microplastics – bioaccumulate in food chain

BPA (Bisphenol A) is used in inner linings of food cans, reusable water bottles, and baby bottles. Long-term exposure leaches BPA into food/drinks. In women: polycystic ovarian syndrome, obesity, recurrent miscarriages.

Climate Change

Greenhouse Effect · Global Warming · Ozone Layer Depletion
Key Topic
What is Climate Change?

Climate change refers to any significant change in climatic factors such as temperature, wind, precipitation, lasting for an unexpected period.

🌡️ Global Warming

Refers specifically to the rise in temperature of Earth.

🌍 Climate Change

Refers to all changes in climate = global warming + changes in rainfall, wind, sea level, etc.

Climate change ≠ Global warming. Global warming is one part of climate change. Climate change = global warming + all other changes.

Causes of Climate Change
  1. Change in the sun's intensity
  2. Slow changes in Earth's orbit around the sun
  3. Change in ocean circulation
  4. Human activities (main cause today):
    • Burning fossil fuels
    • Deforestation
    • Desertification
    • Urbanisation
    • Industrialisation
Effects of Climate Change

Direct Effects

  • Extreme hot and cold spells
  • Wet or dry spells of rainfall

Cascading Effects

  • Adverse effects on ecosystem
Greenhouse Effect & Global Warming

The global average temperature has increased by about 1°C in the last century. The main cause is the abnormal increase in concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere.

🌿 How Greenhouse Effect Works

Think of a car parked in sunlight. Sunlight enters through glass windows (like atmosphere), heats the interior, but the heat cannot escape — the glass traps it. Similarly, greenhouse gases trap heat radiated by Earth.

Steps to Control Global Warming
  1. Reduction in use of fossil fuels for energy generation
  2. Promote non-conventional energy: wind, solar, nuclear
  3. Hydrogen as future energy source
  4. Reduce N₂O emission by minimising nitrogen fertilisers in agriculture
  5. Increase vegetation cover / forests (forests are "sinks" for CO₂ absorption)

Reason for sea level rise = Global warming (melting of glaciers and polar ice).

Ozone Layer

The ozone layer is located in the stratosphere, about 15 to 30 km above Earth's surface. About 90% of atmospheric ozone is concentrated here.

The ozone layer absorbs most of the sun's ultraviolet (UV) radiation and protects life on Earth. It acts like a natural sunscreen — also called the ozone shield or Earth's protective umbrella.

The ozone layer absorbs = Ultraviolet rays. NOT x-rays, infrared rays, or G-rays. Mainly responsible for ozone layer depletion = CFC (Chlorofluorocarbon). CFC used in refrigerators contains Carbon, Hydrogen, Chlorine and Fluorine.

Effects of Ozone Depletion
  • More UV-B radiation (200–280 nm) reaches Earth's surface
  • UV-B affects DNA and photosynthetic chemicals → mutation and cancer
  • Cases of skin cancer (disfigurement) will increase
  • Easy absorption of UV by eye lens and cornea → increase in cataract
  • Destruction of melanin-producing cells → immunosuppression; fair-skinned people at greater risk
  • Decrease in phytoplankton population → affects zooplankton, fish, marine animals, whole aquatic food chain
  • Decrease in yield of crops: corn, rice, soyabean, cotton, bean, pea, wheat
  • Degradation of paints, plastics, polymer materials → economic loss

Introduction to Renewable Energy

Renewable energy = energy from sources that are naturally replenished on a human timescale (sun, wind, water, geothermal heat, biomass). Unlike fossil fuels, they don't run out.

✅ Renewable Sources

  • Wind energy
  • Solar energy
  • Biomass / Biogas
  • Tidal energy
  • Geothermal energy
  • Hydro energy
  • Hydrogen energy
  • Forests and vegetation

❌ Non-Renewable Sources

  • Petroleum
  • Coal
  • Natural gas
  • Fossil fuels
  • Nuclear energy (uranium, plutonium)

For sustainable development, Renewable energy is recommended. By using conventional (fossil fuel) energy → it produces pollution. Solar energy is the only continuously available renewable source.

⚡ Wind Energy

Wind = air in motion, caused by uneven heating of Earth's surface by the sun. The kinetic energy of moving air is proportional to wind speed.

Wind energy is used to run turbines to generate electricity. It is cheap and pollution-free.

Min wind speed: > 6.5 m/sec
Min avg annual wind speed: 15 km/hr
India total wind potential: ~11,000 MW
TN potential: 6000 MW
Gujarat potential: 5000 MW
India harnessed: 2000 MW

Gujarat wind farm locations: Mandvi-Kutch and Lambha near Dwarka. State with maximum wind energy installed capacity = Tamil Nadu.

Wind Mill / Wind Turbine

A machine that converts kinetic energy of wind into mechanical energy of shaft = wind mill. When connected to generator = wind turbine generator / aerogenerator.

Types of Wind Turbines

HAWT (Horizontal Axis Wind Turbine)

  • Generator placed in the nacelle (nessel) — above the tower
  • Large rotor rotates by lift force
  • Blade design = aerodynamic
  • Generator type = Synchronous induction generator
  • Yaw control = rotates rotor in direction of wind

Savonious Wind Turbine

  • Rotor axis is vertical
  • Used for water pumping / low-power applications
  • Nessel is used in Horizontal Axis Wind Turbine (HAWT)
Applications of Wind Energy / Wind Mills

Applications

  • Generate electricity (wind mills)
  • Run water pumps for irrigation
  • Run flour mills (grinding grains)
  • Run machines without electric power
  • Water pumping and irrigation
  • Electro-metallurgical processes
  • Stand-by energy source
  • Run farm machinery
  • Diamond cutting-polishing factory
  • Domestic lighting
  • Hybrid power plants (with solar)
  • Fishing and salt industries
  • Battery charging
  • Telecommunication in remote areas
  • Rural forestry projects
  • Cottage industries

Wind Farm Converts

Wind energy → Electrical energy

✅ Advantages

  • Completely non-polluting
  • Renewable source
  • No raw material required
  • Initial investment moderate
  • Easy and quick construction
  • Low operation and maintenance costs
  • Cheaper generation
  • No greenhouse gases produced
  • No toxic/radioactive waste
  • Land below turbines can grow crops
  • Highly decentralised installation

❌ Limitations

  • Location must have high wind speed (>6.5 m/s)
  • Wind direction continuously changes
  • Motion is variable and unsteady
  • Power production not uniform → intermittent
  • Noise unsuitable for populated areas
  • Only in vast open areas
  • High wind during thunderstorm may damage system
☀️ Solar Energy

Energy from the sun. The most abundant and continuously available renewable energy source.

Solar constant: 1367 W/m²·μm
N-type material produces free electrons for solar rays

Solar Cell / Photovoltaic

N-type semiconductor material produces free electrons when exposed to solar rays. A strip made by connecting two metals is called a thermocouple.

Silicon material: can resist very high temperature, has high boiling point, is very hard.

🌾 Biomass Energy

Biomass = all types of biological substances: plant products (wood, crop, algae, aquatic plants), their residues (straw, husk, sawdust, cow dung, animal droppings), and waste materials (garbage, night soil).

3 Categories of Biomass Resources

1. Traditional Solid Mass

  • Wood
  • Agricultural residue

2. Non-traditional (Liquid)

  • Biomass converted to liquid fuels

3. Fermented Gas

  • Biogas (methane) from fermentation of biomass

Burning 1 kg of wood → 4000–5000 kcal of heat. Burning biomass in presence of air = bioenergy. Residue = manure for agriculture.

Gas with maximum content in biogas = Methane (CH₄). Biogas digestion takes place in absence of Oxygen. Normally Molasses is used to manufacture ethanol.

Biomass Characteristics
  • Huge quantity and renewable
  • Does not produce environmental pollution
  • Gives fuels of high calorific value
  • Available everywhere
  • Very cheap
  • Can be burned → liquid or gaseous fuel
  • Biomass is a carbonic material

✅ Advantages of Biomass Energy

  • Perennial (plants grow continuously)
  • No pollution, no global warming contribution
  • Very cheap, available everywhere
  • Good potential for rural areas
  • Simple technology, unskilled persons can use
  • Methane used as domestic fuel and to run engines
  • Can be grown in nearby seas/lakes (saves land)
  • Gives good quality manures (byproducts as fertiliser)

❌ Disadvantages of Biomass Energy

  • Contains 50–90% water → heavy, difficult to transport
  • Direct combustion produces smoke and smell
  • Very low calorific value if burnt in raw form
  • Biogas plants need large land areas
  • Biogas plants need lots of care and maintenance
  • Cannot be used in urban areas (limited space)
  • Biogas plants may have odour and insect problems
Uses of Biomass Energy
  • Waste organic biomass directly used as domestic fuel
  • Biogas used for heating water
  • Biogas used as domestic fuel in gas stoves (like LPG)
  • Biogas to run engines, boilers, turbines
  • Biogas (methane) to run farm machinery

Pyrolysis = process using dry biomass. Pyrolysis process provides fuel in all forms (solid, liquid, gas). Biomass is a renewable energy source.

🌋 Geothermal Energy

Heat energy from inside the Earth. As depth increases, temperature increases: for every 125 m depth, temperature rises by 1°C. Interior temperature: 600°C to 6000°C.

Rain water penetrates deep → heated by Earth → becomes vapour → comes to surface as hot water springs.

Gujarat hot water springs: Tulsishyam, Lasundra, Tuva
Himachal Pradesh: 5 kW plant at Manikarnam
Ladakh: thermal wells → salt, sulphur, borex
4 Ways Geothermal Energy is Used
1. Hot water method
2. Wet vapour method
3. Dry vapour method
4. Hot rock method
Uses of Geothermal Energy
  • To generate electric power
  • For greenhouses
  • For fisheries
  • In paper industry

✅ Advantages

  • Renewable, available 24/7
  • No fuel required
  • Low operational cost
  • Small land footprint

❌ Limitations

  • Hot water contains sulphur, ammonia, salts → corrosion of pipes
  • Vapour from earth produces sharp noise when released
  • Difficult to obtain sufficient pressure from such vapour
🌊 Tidal Energy

Periodic rise and fall of sea water = tide. Caused by gravitational forces of sun and moon on Earth.

During high tide → water fills tidal basin → turbine gates open when low tide outside → water flows through turbines → electricity generated.

France tidal plant: 230 MW
India (IIT Chennai): 150 MW in Kerala
India sites: Gulf of Kutch, Cambay, Sundarbans
Minimum head required: 5 m

✅ Advantages

  • Power generation is cheaper (no fuel needed)
  • Does not produce harmful waste
  • Pollution-free
  • Power by natural process of tides
  • No raw material required
  • Does not occupy costly land
  • Site can be used for fishing
  • Tidal power is inexhaustible

❌ Disadvantages

  • Power generation not continuous (tide cycles)
  • If insufficient water head → stop generation
  • Only in coastal areas
  • Very low efficiency
  • High capital investment
  • Operational problems during cyclones/storms
  • Costly transmission
  • Heavy silting → frequent dredging needed
💧 Hydrogen Energy

Hydrogen is a clean fuel and the most abundant element in the universe (90% of all atoms). The sun and stars are composed largely of hydrogen. Water is the most abundant compound of hydrogen on Earth.

Hydrogen is considered an alternative fuel due to its ability to power fuel cells in zero-emission electric vehicles. A fuel cell coupled with electric motor is 2–3 times more efficient than internal combustion engine on gasoline.

Fuel Cell

An electrochemical conversion device that converts hydrogen + oxygen → water, producing electricity and heat. Works like a battery but doesn't need recharging. Hydrogen burns in fuel cell → electricity.

Energy in 1 kg of hydrogen ≈ energy in 2.8 kg of gasoline. Hydrogen fuel cell works like a battery, does not require recharging, produces no harmful emissions.

✅ Advantages

  • Readily available
  • No harmful emissions
  • Environmentally friendly
  • Renewable
  • Lighter and smaller unit
  • Can be used as fuel in rockets
  • Higher fuel efficiency

❌ Disadvantages

  • High initial cost
  • Low voltage
  • Low service life
  • Difficult to store
  • Highly flammable
Potential Applications of Hydrogen
  • Production of electricity, heat and water
  • Industrial applications
  • Vehicular transportation
  • Residential applications
  • Commercial applications (telecom towers backup power)
🏞️ Hydro Energy (Brief)

Energy generated by the flow of water. Includes large dams (hydroelectric power plants) and small-scale run-of-the-river plants. Clean, renewable, and reliable. Used widely in India (e.g., Bhakra Nangal, Tehri Dam).
