1. Chemical Equations — Writing & Balancing
What is a Chemical Reaction?

A chemical reaction is a process where atoms rearrange to form new substances. Atoms are NOT destroyed — only rearranged. We can tell a reaction has happened when we see:

  • Change in colour
  • Change in state
  • Evolution of a gas
  • Change in temperature (heat released or absorbed)
  • Formation of a precipitate (insoluble solid)
What is a Chemical Equation?

A chemical equation is a short way to represent a chemical reaction using symbols and formulae instead of writing sentences.

Example: Instead of writing "Magnesium burns in oxygen to form magnesium oxide", we write:

Mg + O₂ → MgO

The substances on the left side (before arrow) = Reactants
The substances on the right side (after arrow) = Products

Rules for Writing a Chemical Equation
  • Reactants written on LHS (Left Hand Side), Products on RHS (Right Hand Side)
  • Separated by + signs. Arrow (→) shows direction of reaction.
  • Physical states: (s) = solid, (l) = liquid, (g) = gas, (aq) = aqueous (dissolved in water)
  • Conditions written above/below the arrow: Heat (Δ), Sunlight, Electricity (⚡), Catalyst (e.g. MnO₂)
🎯 Exam Tip
"(aq)" means dissolved in water. DDCET often asks: which symbol is used for "dissolved in water" = answer is (aq).Yaad rakho — "(aq)" matlab paani ma dissolved chhe. DDCET ma aa 1-mark pakko j aave chhe!
Balanced Chemical Equation
📌 Law of Conservation of Mass
Mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. So total mass of reactants = total mass of products. This means number of atoms of each element must be EQUAL on both sides.

An unbalanced equation is called a skeletal chemical equation.

Skeletal (unbalanced): Mg + O₂ → MgO ← O: 2 on left, 1 on right. WRONG!
Balanced: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO ← Mg: 2 both sides. O: 2 both sides. ✓

The method of balancing is called the hit-and-trial method — we adjust coefficients (numbers before formulae) until both sides match.

⚠ Critical Rule
You can ONLY add coefficients (numbers in front). NEVER change the formula of a compound to balance! For example: to balance oxygen, write "4H₂O" — NOT "H₂O₄" or "(H₂O)₄". Those are completely wrong/different compounds.
Step-by-Step Balancing Example

Balance: Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂

Step 1 — Count atoms: Fe: 1 (LHS) vs 3 (RHS) ← not balanced H: 2 (LHS) vs 2 (RHS) ← ok O: 1 (LHS) vs 4 (RHS) ← not balanced Step 2 — Balance O first (max atoms): add 4 before H₂O: Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂ O: 4 vs 4 ✓, H: 8 vs 2 ← still wrong Step 3 — Balance H: add 4 before H₂: Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂ H: 8 vs 8 ✓ Step 4 — Balance Fe: add 3 before Fe: 3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂ Fe: 3 vs 3 ✓ H: 8 vs 8 ✓ O: 4 vs 4 ✓ ← BALANCED!
🎯 Exam Tip
Always start balancing with the element that appears in the compound with most atoms. End with hydrogen and oxygen. Final check = count all atoms on both sides.Balancing ma pehla most atoms wala element lo, pachhi H ane O. Last ma count karo — barabar chhe kina check karo!

2. Types of Chemical Reactions
Type 1: Combination Reaction — A + B → AB

Two or more substances combine to form a single product. Simple rule: things join together. Many combination reactions are also exothermic.

General Formula: A + B → AB (single product)
1Burning of Magnesium
2Mg(s) + O₂(g) → 2MgO(s)
Mg burns with dazzling WHITE flame. White ash (MgO) forms. Exothermic.
⚠ Mg ribbon must be cleaned with SANDPAPER before burning to remove the oxide layer.
2Quick Lime + Water → Slaked Lime (Slaking of Lime)
CaO(s) + H₂O(l) → Ca(OH)₂(aq) + Heat
Quick lime = CaO. Slaked lime = Ca(OH)₂. Large heat released → Exothermic combination reaction. The beaker becomes HOT to touch.
3Burning of Coal
C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)
Exothermic combination reaction. Asked in DDCET 2024!
4Formation of Water
2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)
Exothermic combination reaction.
5Burning of Natural Gas
CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(g) + Heat
Exothermic combination reaction. LPG/natural gas burning.
Type 2: Decomposition Reaction — AB → A + B

A single compound breaks down into two or more simpler products. It is the opposite of combination reaction. Energy must be supplied (absorbed) — so decomposition reactions are endothermic.

General Formula: AB → A + B (single reactant splits)

Type 3: Displacement Reaction — A + BC → AC + B

A more reactive element displaces (kicks out) a less reactive element from its compound in solution. Think: a stronger player kicks a weaker player out of the team!

General Formula: A + BC → AC + B (more reactive A displaces B)
1Iron + Copper Sulphate (Most important!)
Fe(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → FeSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)
Blue CuSO₄ solution FADES (turns pale). Iron nail becomes REDDISH-BROWN (copper deposits on it). Fe is more reactive than Cu.
2Zinc + Copper Sulphate
Zn(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)
Zn is more reactive than Cu → displaces it.
3Lead + Copper Chloride
Pb(s) + CuCl₂(aq) → PbCl₂(aq) + Cu(s)
Pb is more reactive than Cu.
📌 Rule
More reactive metal ALWAYS displaces less reactive metal. Reactivity: Zn > Fe > Pb > [H] > Cu.Vadhare reactive metal ochi reactive ne hatave chhe. Zn > Fe > Cu — aa order pakko yaad rakhvo! So Zn can displace Fe; Fe can displace Cu; but Cu CANNOT displace Fe.
Type 4: Double Displacement Reaction — AB + CD → AD + CB

Two compounds exchange their ions to form two new compounds. Think: two couples swapping dance partners! Usually produces a precipitate (↓) or water.

General Formula: AB + CD → AD + CB (ion exchange)
1Sodium Sulphate + Barium Chloride (Precipitation)
Na₂SO₄(aq) + BaCl₂(aq) → BaSO₄(s)↓ + 2NaCl(aq)
WHITE precipitate of BaSO₄ forms. Both solutions are colourless. Classic example of precipitation reaction.
2NaCl + AgNO₃ (Precipitation)
NaCl(aq) + AgNO₃(aq) → AgCl(s)↓ + NaNO₃(aq)
WHITE precipitate of AgCl forms.
3Lead Nitrate + Potassium Iodide
Pb(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2KI(aq) → PbI₂(s)↓ + 2KNO₃(aq)
YELLOW precipitate of PbI₂ forms. Lead nitrate = colourless; KI = colourless.
4Neutralisation (also Double Displacement)
NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
Acid + Base → Salt + Water. This is also a type of double displacement reaction.
📌 Precipitate Colours — Must Know!
AgCl = WHITE   BaSO₄ = WHITE   PbI₂ = YELLOW

📌 OIL RIG — Memory TrickOIL RIG = Oxidation Is Loss (electrons), Reduction Is Gain. Aa trick thi yaad thashe!!
Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons)  |  Reduction Is Gain (of electrons)
Redox Reaction = Oxidation + Reduction happening simultaneously
1CuO + H₂ → Redox Reaction
CuO(s) + H₂(g) →Δ Cu(s) + H₂O(l)
H₂ GAINS oxygen → H₂ is OXIDISED (black CuO turns brown/copper coloured)
CuO LOSES oxygen → CuO is REDUCED
Both happen at the same time = REDOX reaction
2Zn + CuO
Zn(s) + CuO(s) → ZnO(s) + Cu(s)
Zn gains oxygen → Zn is OXIDISED. CuO loses oxygen → CuO is REDUCED.
3ZnO + C (from Ch-3 Extraction — Redox)
ZnO(s) + C(s) → Zn(s) + CO(g)
C gains oxygen → C is OXIDISED. ZnO loses oxygen → ZnO is REDUCED. Carbon acts as a REDUCING AGENT.
4MnO₂ + HCl (NCERT redox example)
MnO₂(s) + 4HCl(aq) → MnCl₂(aq) + 2H₂O(l) + Cl₂(g)
HCl is OXIDISED to Cl₂ (loses H, gains no O but loses electrons). MnO₂ is REDUCED to MnCl₂.
5Copper Oxidation
2Cu(s) + O₂(g) →Δ 2CuO(s)
Cu gains oxygen → Cu is OXIDISED. Black CuO coating forms on copper surface.
📌 Rusting of Iron = Redox Reaction
Fe + O₂ + H₂O → Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (rust). Iron is OXIDISED (gains oxygen).

When Mg burns in air: Mg is OXIDISED (gains oxygen → MgO). White ash (MgO) is collected in a watch-glass. This is a combination AND oxidation reaction.Mg bale toh MgO baney chhe (white ash). Aa combination ane oxidation — banne ek j reaction ma!

TypeEnergyExamples

✏ CORRECTION — Respiration Equation
Your original notes had this BACKWARDS. The correct equation is:

C₆H₁₂O₆(aq) + 6O₂(aq) → 6CO₂(aq) + 6H₂O(l) + energy

Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy. This is EXOTHERMIC.
The reversed version (6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂) is PHOTOSYNTHESIS — NOT respiration!
📌 Key Examples to Remember
  • Barium hydroxide + Ammonium chloride → Temperature DROPS (endothermic)
  • Dissolution of NaOH in water → Temperature RISES (exothermic)
  • Slaking of lime (CaO + H₂O) → Very exothermic (beaker becomes very hot)
  • Decomposition of AgCl in sunlight → Endothermic (absorbs light energy)
5. Corrosion & Rancidity
Corrosion

Corrosion is the slow damage of metals due to reaction with air, moisture, acids, etc. It is essentially an oxidation reaction.

Fe(s) + O₂(g) + H₂O(l) → Fe₂O₃·xH₂O(s) ← Rust (hydrated iron oxide, reddish-brown)
MetalCorrosion ProductColour of Coating
Iron (Fe)Rust = Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (hydrated iron oxide)Reddish-brown flaky layer
Silver (Ag)Silver sulphide (Ag₂S)Black coating (tarnishing)
Copper (Cu)Basic copper carbonate [Cu(OH)₂·CuCO₃]Green coating (patina)

Prevention of Corrosion:

  • Painting / Oiling / Greasing — creates a barrier between metal and air/moisture
  • Galvanisation — coating iron/steel with zinc. Even if zinc coating breaks, zinc (more reactive) corrodes first, protecting iron.
  • Alloying — e.g. stainless steel (Fe+Ni+Cr) does not rust
Rancidity

When fats/oils in food are oxidised on exposure to air, they become rancid — their smell and taste change. This is called rancidity.

  • Antioxidants — added to food to prevent oxidation
  • Airtight containers — slow down oxidation
  • Nitrogen gas flushing — chips manufacturers flush packets with inert N₂ gas to prevent oxidation of fats. N₂ does not react with food.
  • Refrigeration — lower temperature slows down oxidation
🎯 Exam Tip
Why do chips bags have nitrogen? To prevent oxidation (rancidity). Nitrogen is an inert (inactive) gas.Chips na bag ma nitrogen hoy chhe tena mate kema? Oxidation rokva! N₂ inert chhe — react nathi kartu.

1. Acids — Definition & Properties
What is an Acid?

An acid is a substance that produces H⁺ (hydrogen ions) in aqueous (water) solution. Acids work only in water — without water, no H⁺ ions form → no acidic behaviour.

HCl + H₂O → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) ← dissolves in water → acidic
📌 Hydronium Ion
H⁺ ions cannot exist alone — they combine with water molecules to form Hydronium ions (H₃O⁺).
H⁺ + H₂O → H₃O⁺
So H⁺(aq) and H₃O⁺ mean the same thing. Both representations are correct.

Strong vs Weak Acids

Strong acid = produces MORE H⁺ ions per molecule = lower pH. Example: HCl (hydrochloric acid) — almost completely ionised.

Weak acid = produces FEWER H⁺ ions = higher pH. Example: CH₃COOH (acetic acid) — only partially ionised.

2. Bases & Alkalis — Definition & Properties
What is a Base?

A base is a substance that produces OH⁻ (hydroxide ions) in aqueous solution.

NaOH(s) →(H₂O) Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) ← OH⁻ ions responsible for basic behaviour
📌 Base vs Alkali
ALKALI = a base that dissolves in water. All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis.
Examples of alkalis: NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂, NH₄OH
Mg(OH)₂ does not fully dissolve in water — it is a base but less soluble as an alkali.

⚠ Why NOT store sour food in brass/copper vessels?
Sour food contains acids (e.g. citric acid, tartaric acid). These acids react with brass (Cu+Zn) or copper, dissolving the metal into the food. This can make food toxic and changes its taste. Use glass, steel, or ceramic vessels for sour food.
⚠ Dilution Safety Rule
Always add ACID to WATER, never water to acid. Heat generated during dilution is large — adding water to acid can cause the mixture to splash, causing severe burns. Mnemonic: "Do as you oughta — add acid to wata!"
5. Acids & Bases in Water Solution
Why Water is Needed for Acidic/Basic Behaviour
HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻ ← H⁺ ions form ONLY in presence of water
NaOH →(H₂O) Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) ← OH⁻ ions form only in water
⚠ DRY HCl gas — Very Common Exam Question!
Dry HCl gas does NOT change the colour of dry litmus paper.
Why? Because without water, HCl cannot ionise to produce H⁺ ions. No H⁺ = no acidic behaviour = litmus unchanged.
Wet/Moist HCl DOES change litmus to red because water is present → H⁺ ions form.
Conclusion: Water is ESSENTIAL for acidic behaviour.
Distilled Water vs Rain Water

CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid) → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ → ions present → conducts electricity.
Rain water is slightly acidic (pH ≈ 5.6) for this reason.

7. Importance of pH in Everyday Life
1. pH and Living Beings / Aquatic Life
  • Our body works normally only within a narrow pH range of 7.0 to 7.8. Even small changes are fatal.
  • Blood pH = 7.4 (slightly alkaline). Maintained by buffer systems in the body.
  • Enzymes work at specific pH — deviation → enzyme stops working → digestion/respiration affected.
  • Fish and aquatic animals survive only if water pH is near 7.
  • Acid rain: pH of rainwater < 5.6 = acid rain. Lowers river/lake pH → kills aquatic life, damages buildings, corrodes statues.

2. pH in Soil
  • Ideal soil pH = 6 to 7 (slightly acidic to neutral) for healthy plant growth.
  • Too acidic soil → add quicklime (CaO) or slaked lime Ca(OH)₂ or chalk (CaCO₃) to neutralise.
  • Too basic/alkaline soil → add organic matter/compost or acidic fertilisers to reduce pH.

  • Stomach secretes hydrochloric acid (HCl). Stomach pH ≈ 1.5 to 2 (strongly acidic).
  • HCl helps digest food and kills harmful bacteria swallowed with food.
  • Excess HCl → indigestion / pain / acidity.

4. How Metals & Non-Metals React — Ionic Bond
Formation of Ionic Bond

Metals lose electrons → become positive ions (CATIONS). Non-metals gain electrons → become negative ions (ANIONS). The strong electrostatic attraction between opposite charges = IONIC BOND.

Na → Na⁺ + e⁻ [2,8,1 → 2,8 configuration → stable octet. Loses 1 electron]
Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻ [2,8,7 → 2,8,8 configuration → stable octet. Gains 1 electron]
Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl [NaCl = ionic compound — NOT molecules, but aggregate of ions]
📌 MgCl₂ formation
Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻ (loses 2 electrons)
2Cl + 2e⁻ → 2Cl⁻ (each gains 1 electron)
Mg²⁺ + 2Cl⁻ → MgCl₂ (ionic compound)

Ionic compounds do NOT exist as individual molecules. They exist as an orderly 3D arrangement (crystal lattice) of many ions held by electrostatic forces.

5. Properties of Ionic Compounds
PropertyDetail & Reason
Physical StateAlways SOLIDS at room temperature. Hard but BRITTLE — break into pieces when pressure applied (planes of ions shift, like charges face each other → repulsion → break).
Melting & Boiling PointsVery HIGH. Strong ionic forces need large energy to break. e.g. NaCl melts at 1074K, CaO at 2850K.
SolubilityGenerally soluble in WATER. Insoluble in non-polar solvents like kerosene, petrol, alcohol.
Electrical Conductivity — SOLIDDo NOT conduct electricity. Ions are FIXED in a rigid crystal lattice — cannot move → no current flows.
Electrical Conductivity — MOLTEN or AQUEOUSDO conduct electricity. Ions become FREE to move → carry current. This is why salt solution conducts electricity.

Thermit reaction = Fe₂O₃ + 2Al → 2Fe + Al₂O₃ + Heat. Used to JOIN RAILWAY TRACKS. Very frequently asked in DDCET!
Electrolytic Refining

Method to obtain pure metals. Used for Cu, Zn, Sn, Ni, Ag, Au.

  • Anode: Impure metal
  • Cathode: Thin strip of PURE metal
  • Electrolyte: Solution of metal salt
  • Pure metal from anode dissolves → deposits on cathode. Insoluble impurities settle as ANODE MUD.
➕ Iron Pillar of Delhi — Famous NCERT Fact
The iron pillar near Qutub Minar, Delhi was built more than 1600 years ago. It is 8m high, weighs 6 tonnes. Remarkably, it has NOT rusted despite being exposed to air and water for 1600 years. Ancient Indian metallurgists had developed a special process to prevent rusting. It contains a very thin layer of a special compound (misawite) on the surface that prevents rusting. This is a testament to ancient Indian metallurgy.
7. Alloys

An ALLOY is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals, OR a metal and a non-metal. Properties of metals can be improved by making alloys.

AlloyCompositionProperties & Use

📌 Properties of Alloys vs Pure Metals
  • Alloys have LOWER electrical conductivity than pure metals
  • Alloys have LOWER melting point than the pure metals they contain
  • This is why brass and bronze are not used for electrical wires (copper is used instead)
📌 Gold Carats
  • 24 carat gold = 100% pure gold. Very soft — not suitable for jewellery.
  • 22 carat gold = 22 parts pure gold + 2 parts Cu or Ag. Used for jewellery in India. Harder than 24 carat.
➕ Why food cans are coated with TIN, not ZINC?
Food cans (e.g. canned vegetables, tomatoes) are coated with TIN (Sn) on the inside.
Why not zinc? Zinc is MORE REACTIVE than tin. If the zinc coating is broken, zinc reacts with the acidic food content → forms harmful zinc compounds → food becomes toxic.
Why tin? Tin is LESS REACTIVE. Even if coating breaks, tin reacts very slowly with food → safer for food storage.
🎯 Exam Tip
Brass = Cu+Zn, Bronze = Cu+Sn, Solder = Pb+Sn (low MP for welding), Stainless Steel = Fe+Ni+Cr (no rust). These alloy compositions are very common DDCET MCQs!
8. Corrosion & Prevention

Corrosion = slow damage of metals due to reaction with substances around them (air, moisture, acids). Corrosion is essentially an oxidation reaction.

MetalCorrosion ProductColour
Iron (Fe)Rust = Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (hydrated iron oxide)Reddish-brown flaky layer — weakens iron
Silver (Ag)Silver sulphide (Ag₂S)Black coating (tarnishing)
Copper (Cu)Basic copper carbonate [Cu(OH)₂·CuCO₃]Green coating (patina)
⚠ Conditions Required for Iron to Rust
Iron rusts ONLY when BOTH oxygen (air) AND water (moisture) are present together.
• Only water (no air) = NO rust
• Only dry air (no water) = NO rust
• Both air + water together = RUST FORMS
This was proved by the test-tube experiment (tubes A, B, C).
Prevention of Corrosion
MethodHow it works
Painting / Oiling / GreasingCreates a physical barrier — prevents contact of metal with air and moisture.
GalvanisationCoating iron/steel with a thin layer of ZINC. Even if zinc coating breaks — zinc (MORE REACTIVE) corrodes first, protecting iron. Called sacrificial protection.
Chrome plating / ElectroplatingCoating iron with a less reactive metal (chromium, tin). Tin cans safer than zinc-coated for food (tin less reactive).
AnodisingMaking a thick protective oxide layer on ALUMINIUM by electrolysis. Al article is made anode → oxygen evolved at anode → reacts with Al → thick Al₂O₃ layer forms → corrosion resistant. Layer can be dyed for attractive finish.
AlloyingMixing metals changes properties. Stainless steel (Fe+Ni+Cr) does not rust at all.
🎯 Exam Tip
Galvanisation = coating with ZINC. Zinc is MORE REACTIVE than iron → zinc corrodes first (zinc oxidises before iron does) → iron is protected. This is called "sacrificial protection."
