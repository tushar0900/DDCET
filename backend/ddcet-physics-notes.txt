1.3  Vernier Caliper
Invented by: Pierre Vernier (French mathematician) in 1631. Measures to 0.1 mm or 0.05 mm or 0.02 mm precision.

Parts
Part
Function
Main Scale
Fixed scale — upper in inches, lower in cm
Vernier Scale
Small sliding scale on main scale

Measure outer dimensions (length, diameter)

Measure inner dimensions (bore, slot width)

Measures depth of holes or slots

Principle & Least Count
N divisions of Vernier Scale = (N−1) divisions of Main Scale
LC = 1 MSD − 1 VSD  =  (1/N) × MSD
Where N = number of vernier divisions, MSD = main scale division value
For standard vernier: 1 MSD = 1 mm, N = 10  →  LC = 0.1 mm = 0.01 cm
Reading = Main Scale Reading (MSR) + (Vernier Coincidence × LC)

EXAMPLE: Taking a Vernier Reading
MSR = 2.3 cm. The 7th vernier division coincides with a main scale mark. LC = 0.01 cm.
Solution: Reading = 2.3 + (7 × 0.01) = 2.3 + 0.07 = 2.37 cm

Zero Error
Type
Condition
Correction

Positive zero error

Negative zero error

1.4  Micrometer Screw Gauge
Purpose: Measures thickness of thin wire, paper, sheet, blade. More precise than Vernier — measures to 0.01 mm = 0.001 cm.
Principle: Linear displacement of spindle is proportional to angular displacement. One full rotation = one pitch distance.

Parts
Part
Function

Pitch and Least Count
Pitch Distance: Distance between two consecutive threads = distance spindle moves in one full rotation. Typical: 0.5 mm.

LC = Pitch / Total circular divisions = 0.5 mm / 50 = 0.01 mm = 0.001 cm
Reading = Barrel (Main Scale) Reading + (Thimble reading × LC)

EXAMPLE: Taking a Micrometer Reading
Barrel reads 7.5 mm (7 mm marks visible + 0.5 mm mark below datum line). Thimble: 24th division aligns with datum. LC = 0.01 mm.
Solution: Reading = 7.5 + (24 × 0.01) = 7.5 + 0.24 = 7.74 mm

1.5  Errors in Measurement
No measurement is ever perfect. The difference between true value and measured value is the error.

Types of Errors
Type
Direction
Cause
Minimized by
Systematic Error
Always one direction (+ or −)
Known: instrument defects, wrong technique, personal habits, external conditions
Improving technique, better instruments
Random Error
Unpredictable (+ or −)
Unknown: random variations in temperature, pressure, voltage, vibrations
Repeating observations many times and taking arithmetic mean

Estimation of Errors (Step by Step)
Let a quantity be measured n times, values: a₁, a₂, a₃, ..., aₙ

Mean Value:  ā = (a₁ + a₂ + ... + aₙ) / n
Arithmetic mean = best (true) value
Absolute Error (each):  Δaᵢ = ā − aᵢ
Can be positive or negative
Mean Absolute Error:  Δā = (|Δa₁| + |Δa₂| + ... + |Δaₙ|) / n
Always positive — take magnitudes
Final Result:  a = ā ± Δā
True value lies between ā − Δā and ā + Δā
Relative Error = Δā / ā     |     Percentage Error = (Δā / ā) × 100 %

EXAMPLE: Refractive index measurements
Values: 1.29, 1.33, 1.34, 1.35, 1.32, 1.36, 1.30, 1.33
Solution: Mean ā = 10.62/8 = 1.33 | Absolute errors: 0.04, 0.00, 0.01, 0.02, 0.01, 0.03, 0.03, 0.00 | Sum = 0.14 | Δā = 0.14/8 = 0.0175 ≈ 0.02 | Relative error = 0.02/1.33 = ±0.015 | Percentage error = ±1.5%

Propagation of Errors
Operation
Error Rule

Solution: Cp = 3.7 μF | ΔCp = ±(0.1+0.2) = ±0.3 μF | % error = 0.3/3.7 × 100 = ±8.1%
EXAMPLE: Difference of lengths
l₁ = (5.62 ± 0.01) cm, l₂ = (4.34 ± 0.02) cm

Equations of Motion — Uniform Acceleration
⚠  Valid ONLY when: (1) acceleration a is constant, (2) motion is in a straight line.

1st Equation:   v = u + at
v = final velocity, u = initial velocity, a = acceleration, t = time
2nd Equation:   s = ut + ½at²
s = displacement covered in time t
3rd Equation:   v² = u² + 2as
Use when time t is not given or not needed
4th Equation:   s = (u + v) × t / 2
Use when both u and v are known; average velocity × time

★  For FREE FALL under gravity: a = g = 9.8 m/s² (downward). For body dropped from rest: u = 0. For body thrown upward: v = 0 at highest point.

EXAMPLE: Car starts from rest, reaches 72 km/h in 10 s
Find: (a) acceleration, (b) distance
Solution: u=0, v=72×5/18=20 m/s, t=10s | a=(20-0)/10 = 2 m/s² | s=0×10+½×2×100 = 100 m
EXAMPLE: Ball thrown upward at 20 m/s
Find: (a) time to reach top, (b) maximum height
Solution: (a) v=0, 0=20-9.8t → t=2.04 s | (b) 0²=20²-2×9.8×h → h=400/19.6 = 20.4 m
EXAMPLE: Brakes applied to car at 30 m/s; stops in 100 m
Find retardation
Solution: v=0, u=30, s=100 | 0=900+2a×100 → a = -4.5 m/s² (retardation = 4.5 m/s²)

2.2  Linear Momentum and Impulse
Linear Momentum (p): Product of mass and velocity. Vector — direction same as velocity.
p = m × v      SI unit: kg ms⁻¹  |  CGS unit: g cm s⁻¹

Condition
Relationship
m constant
p ∝ v (momentum proportional to velocity)
v constant
p ∝ m (momentum proportional to mass)
Two bodies, equal momentum
M₁v₁ = M₂v₂  →  v₂/v₁ = M₁/M₂ (heavier body: smaller velocity)

Impulse of Force
Impulse: Large force acting for a very short time. Impulse = change in momentum.
Impulse = F × t = Δp = m·v₂ − m·v₁
SI unit: N·s (Ns) or kg ms⁻¹  |  CGS: dyne·second  |  Vector quantity

Situation
How impulse principle applies
Cricket player lowers hands while catching ball
Increases time → reduces force → hands not hurt
Person falls on sand/cushion (not cement floor)
Sand yields → longer time → smaller force → less injury
Glasswares wrapped in paper for packing
Paper increases impact time → reduces average force → less breakage
Shock absorbers in vehicles
Increases time of impact → reduces jerk force on rider
Train bogies provided with buffers
Buffers increase impact time → reduce jerks during shunting

2.3  Circular Motion
Angular Velocity (ω): Angle described by rotating body per unit time. Denoted ω (omega). Unit: radians/second (rad/s).
ω = θ/t = 2π/T = 2πf
θ = angle in radians, T = time period, f = frequency (Hz)
v = rω    (linear velocity = radius × angular velocity)
r = radius of circular path
Angular acceleration:  α = (ω₂ − ω₁)/t  [rad/s²]
Linear acceleration:   a = rα

★  All particles in a rotating body have the SAME angular velocity ω, but different linear velocities v = rω. Particles farther from axis move faster.

Banking of Roads

Minimum radius for safe turn on flat road:  r = v²/(μg)
μ = coefficient of friction
★  Maximum safe speed does NOT depend on mass of vehicle — only on radius, banking angle, and g.

2.4  Work, Energy and Power
Work
Work: Done when force causes displacement in the direction of force. Scalar quantity.
W = F × d × cos θ     SI unit: Joule (J) = N·m     CGS: erg = dyne·cm
1 J = 10⁷ erg. Work = 0 if F=0, d=0, or θ=90°

Kinetic and Potential Energy
Kinetic Energy:  KE = ½mv²
m = mass, v = velocity
Potential Energy:  PE = mgh
h = height above reference, g = 9.8 m/s²
Work-Energy Theorem:  W = ΔKE = ½mv² − ½mu²
Conservation of Energy:  KE + PE = constant  (no non-conservative forces)

Power
Power: Rate of doing work.
P = W/t = F × v     SI unit: Watt (W) = J/s
1 horsepower = 746 W | 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J (commercial unit)

🔑 KEY FORMULAS TO REMEMBER
	•	v=u+at | s=ut+½at² | v²=u²+2as | s=(u+v)t/2
	•	Free fall: a=g=9.8 m/s². Body dropped from rest: u=0. At top: v=0.
	•	1 N = 10⁵ dyne | Impulse = F×t = Δp = m(v₂−v₁)
	•	v = rω | a = rα | ω = 2πf = 2π/T
	•	Centripetal F = mv²/r = mω²r (inward, real force)
	•	At lowest point: T = mg + mv²/r | At highest: T = mv²/r − mg
	•	KE = ½mv² | PE = mgh | P = W/t = Fv

3.2  Coulomb's Law
Coulomb's Law: The electric force between two stationary point charges is directly proportional to the product of their charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
F = k × q₁q₂ / r²      k = 1/(4πε₀) = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²·C⁻²
ε₀ = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² C²/Nm² (permittivity of free space)
In a medium:  Fₘ = F/εᵣ = F/K     where K = dielectric constant > 1
So Fₘ < F — force is always less in a medium than in vacuum

Change
Effect on Force
Distance doubled (r → 2r)
Force becomes 1/4th (∝ 1/r²)
Charges doubled (q₁,q₂ → 2q₁,2q₂)
Force becomes 4 times
Medium introduced (εᵣ > 1)
Force decreases (Fₘ < F)

EXAMPLE: Total charge on 20 extra electrons

Solution: q = ne = 20 × (−1.6×10⁻¹⁹) = −3.2 × 10⁻¹⁸ C

3.3  Electric Field, Potential and Flux
Electric Field (E)
Electric Field: Force per unit positive test charge at a point. Shows the effect of a charge in surrounding space.
E = F/q₀ = kQ/r² = Q/(4πε₀r²)      SI unit: N/C or V/m     VECTOR quantity

Properties of Electric Field Lines
Property
1. Start from positive charges, end at negative charges
2. Tangent at any point = direction of field at that point
3. Two field lines NEVER intersect
4. Denser lines = stronger field; spaced-out lines = weaker field
5. Uniform field lines are parallel and equidistant
6. Field lines are imaginary; electric field is real
7. Always perpendicular to conducting surface
8. Do NOT form closed paths

Electric Potential (V)
Electric Potential: Work done against the electric field to bring unit positive charge from infinity to a point in the field.
V = W/q₀ = kQ/r      Unit: Volt (V) = Joule/Coulomb     SCALAR quantity
Potential Difference:  VB − VA = −WAB/q₀

Electric Flux (φ)
Electric Flux: Number of electric field lines passing perpendicularly through a given surface area.
φ = E · A · cos θ      SI unit: V·m or N·m²/C     SCALAR quantity
θ = angle between field E and normal to surface. θ=0°: φ=EA (max). θ=90°: φ=0.

Electric Current (I): Rate of flow of electric charge. I = Q/t. SI unit: Ampere (A). 1 A = 1 C/s.
Ohm's Law: At constant temperature, current through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across it.
V = I × R      R = V/I      I = V/R
V = Voltage (V), I = Current (A), R = Resistance (Ω)

Resistance and Resistivity
R = ρ × L / A
ρ (rho) = resistivity (Ω·m), L = length (m), A = cross-sectional area (m²)

3.5  Capacitance and Capacitors
Capacitance (C): Ability to store electric charge. Ratio of charge stored to potential difference. C = Q/V
C = Q / V      SI unit: Farad (F) = Coulomb/Volt
1 μF = 10⁻⁶ F | 1 nF = 10⁻⁹ F | 1 pF = 10⁻¹² F

Parallel Plate Capacitor
C = ε₀ × A / d         (vacuum/air as dielectric)
ε₀ = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² F/m (absolute permittivity)
C = K × ε₀ × A / d    (with dielectric material of constant K)
K = εᵣ = dielectric constant. K > 1 always.
★  Capacitance increases if: plate area A increases, gap d decreases, or dielectric constant K increases. Condition: d² << A (gap much smaller than plate dimensions).

4.5  Thermal Conductivity
Coefficient of Thermal Conductivity (K): Rate of heat flow per unit area per unit temperature gradient. Depends only on material.
ΔQ/Δt = K × A × (T₁ − T₂) / d
ΔQ/Δt = rate of heat flow (W) | A = area (m²) | T₁-T₂ = temp. diff. | d = thickness (m)
SI unit of K: W m⁻¹ K⁻¹ = J s⁻¹ m⁻¹ K⁻¹      CGS unit: cal s⁻¹ cm⁻¹ °C⁻¹
Steady State: When temperature of every cross-section of rod becomes constant. No more absorption of heat — only transmission. Specific heat has no role; only thermal conductivity matters.

EXAMPLE: Copper rod
L=19 cm=0.19 m, A=785×10⁻⁷ m², T₁=100°C, T₂=30°C, t=600 s, K=380 W/mK
Solution: Q = 380×785×10⁻⁷×70×600/0.19 = 6594 J = 6.594 kJ
EXAMPLE: Glass window
A=0.5 m², d=6×10⁻³ m, T₁=23°C, T₂=2°C, t=3600 s, K=1.0 W/mK
Solution: Q = 1×0.5×21×3600/0.006 = 6,300,000 J = 63×10⁵ J

Linear, Area and Volume Thermal Expansion
Linear:   L = L₀(1 + α·ΔT)    →    ΔL = L₀ × α × ΔT
α = coefficient of linear expansion (°C⁻¹ or K⁻¹)
Area:     A = A₀(1 + β·ΔT)    β = 2α  (superficial expansion)
Volume:   V = V₀(1 + γ·ΔT)    γ = 3α  (cubical expansion)
Relationship:  α : β : γ = 1 : 2 : 3
★  Bimetallic strips bend when heated because two metals have different α values — one expands more, causing the strip to curve. Used in thermostats, bimetallic thermometers.

5.4  Total Internal Reflection (TIR)
Total Internal Reflection (TIR): When light travels from denser to rarer medium and angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle, ALL light reflects back into the denser medium.

nᵢ = refractive index of incident (denser) medium | nᵣ = refractive index of rarer medium

θc = sin⁻¹(1/2.42) ≈ 24°  (smallest — most TIR)

5.7  Echo, Reverberation and Sabine's Formula
Echo
Echo: Repetition of sound heard after reflection from a hard surface. Source and reflected sound heard as separate sounds.
Minimum distance for echo = v × t_min / 2 = 343 × 0.1 / 2 ≈ 17 m
Minimum persistence of hearing = 1/10 s = 0.1 s. Speed of sound ≈ 343 m/s at room temp.

Reverberation
Reverberation: Persistence of sound in an enclosed space after source stops — due to multiple reflections from walls, ceiling, floor.
Reverberation Time (RT): Time for sound intensity to fall to 10⁻⁶ of original value after source stops. Equivalently: time for 60 dB drop in sound level.
Application
Optimal Reverberation Time
Speech/Lectures
0.5 – 1.0 seconds
Music (orchestral)
1.5 – 2.5 seconds
Too high RT effect
Sounds blend, speech unclear, echoing
Too low RT effect
Sounds dead, flat, poor acoustics

Sabine's Formula
T = 0.161 × V / A
T = reverberation time (s) | V = volume of room (m³) | A = total absorption (sabins)
Total Absorption:  A = Σ(aᵢ × Sᵢ)
aᵢ = absorption coefficient of ith surface | Sᵢ = area of ith surface (m²)
Absorption Coefficient:  a = Sound energy absorbed / Sound energy incident
Range: 0 (perfect reflection) to 1 (perfect absorption). 1 sabin = 1 m² of perfectly absorbing surface.
